POSTPARTUM COMPLICATIONS


Postpartum complications refer to medical issues or conditions that can arise after childbirth.
These complications can vary in severity and may affect different aspects of a woman's health.




Postpartum complications: What you need to know

After childbirth, you're likely focused on caring for your baby. But health problems,
some life-threatening, can happen in the weeks and months afterward and many aren't
aware of the warning signs. Here's what you need to know about postpartum complications.



A growing problem

A pregnancy-related death is the death of a woman while pregnant or within one year of the end
of a pregnancy. More than half the pregnancy-related deaths happen after childbirth.

According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), the number of reported pregnancy-related deaths
in the United States in 2014 was 18 deaths per 100,000 live births. That's up from 7.2 deaths per 100,000 live births
in 1987. Research also shows racial disparities. From 2011 to 2014, the pregnancy-related mortality ratios for black
women were more than three times higher than for white women.



Some common postpartum complications include:

  • Postpartum hemorrhage: Excessive bleeding following childbirth.

  • Infections: Infections can occur in the uterus (endometritis), urinary tract (urinary tract infection), or surgical site
    (wound infection) after a cesarean section. These infections can cause symptoms like fever, pain, and abnormal discharge.

  • Postpartum depression: A mood disorder characterized by feelings of sadness, anxiety, and exhaustion that
    persist beyond the "baby blues" period. It can affect a woman's ability to care for herself and her baby.

  • Mastitis: Inflammation of the breast tissue, usually caused by a bacterial infection. It can lead to breast pain, swelling, redness,
    and flu-like symptoms.

  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE): Blood clots that can form in the legs (DVT)
    and travel to the lungs (PE). These conditions can cause pain, swelling, and difficulty breathing.

  • Perineal tears: Tears or lacerations that occur in the perineum (the area between the vagina and anus)
    during childbirth. The severity of tears can vary, ranging from minor to more extensive tears that
    require repair.

  • Preeclampsia: A condition characterized by high blood pressure and organ damage, usually occurring
    after the 20th week of pregnancy. It can lead to complications such as seizures (eclampsia) and organ failure.


    • Prevention tips

    It is now recommended by the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists that postpartum care be continuous
    rather than limited to a single appointment following delivery. Make an appointment with your physician during the first three
    weeks following birth. See your healthcare physician for a thorough postpartum examination within 12 weeks of giving birth.
    Speak with your provider if scheduling an appointment is becoming difficult for you. Ask your friends and relatives for assistance
    with child care.

    Your doctor will evaluate your emotional state and mood, talk about birth spacing and contraception, go over information regarding
    feeding and caring for your baby, ask about your sleep patterns and fatigue-related problems, and perform a physical examination
    during this visit. This may involve examining your uterus, vagina, cervix, and abdomen to make sure you're recovering properly. You
    should use this opportunity to discuss any worries you may have, such as starting up again or adjusting to life with a new child.

    Additionally, make sure to mention the date of your delivery to each medical professional you meet in the year after childbirth. This
    can assist your healthcare professional in determining whether your symptoms are connected to your recent pregnancy.
